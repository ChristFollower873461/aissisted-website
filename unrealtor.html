Real estate jargon glossary

Simple words for the terms that hide the money.

An alphabetized buyer-side decoder for listing copy, agent texts, FSBO conversations, builder offers, loan documents, contract counters, and inspection negotiations.

How to use this

If a term changes your deadline, cash to close, cancellation right, or risk of losing earnest money, treat it like a decision point. Ask for the written version.

Appraisal gap
The cash gap you may cover if the appraisal is lower than the price. The cap is the important part.
As-is
Often means the seller prefers not to repair. It does not automatically erase every inspection right. The contract controls.
Builder incentive
A credit, upgrade, buydown, or discount from a builder. Ask what lender, title, deposit, and cancellation strings are attached.
Buyer agency agreement
A contract that can make you responsible for paying an agent. Check exclusivity, duration, cancellation, and compensation before touring.
Cash to close
The money you need at closing after down payment, costs, prepaids, credits, deposits, and adjustments are counted.
Clear to close
Lender language meaning underwriting conditions are mostly satisfied. It is not the same as “you own the house.”
Closing Disclosure
The final mortgage disclosure you normally receive at least three business days before closing. Compare it to the Loan Estimate line by line.
Comps / CMA
Comparable sales and a comparative market analysis. Translation: the evidence people use to argue what the house is worth.
Contingency
A condition that protects you if inspection, financing, appraisal, title, insurance, or another core issue fails.
Discount points
Upfront money paid for a lower rate. The question is not “lower rate good?” It is “how many months until I break even?”
DOM / CDOM
Days on market and cumulative days on market. Long time on market may mean leverage, bad pricing, condition issues, or stale listing strategy.
DTI
Debt-to-income ratio. Monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income. It can decide whether the loan survives underwriting.
Dual agency
One brokerage sits near both sides of the transaction. Get duties, conflicts, and consent in writing.
Due diligence period
Your window to investigate the property and decide whether to proceed, renegotiate, or cancel under the contract.
Earnest money
Your good-faith deposit. It can be protected inside contingencies and at risk after missed deadlines.
Escalation clause
A clause that raises your offer above competing offers up to a cap. It needs proof rules and a firm ceiling.
Escrow holdback
Money held after closing for specific work. It needs scope, amount, deadline, release rules, and lender/title approval.
FSBO
For Sale By Owner. The seller is not using a listing agent, so paperwork, deadlines, access, title, and negotiation need extra structure.
Inspection contingency
Your right to inspect, object, renegotiate, or cancel if the contract allows it. The deadline and notice method matter.
IPC limit
Interested party contribution limit. The lender's cap on seller, builder, or agent-paid credits.
Leaseback / rent-back
The seller stays after closing. It needs rent, deposit, insurance, deadline, damage rules, and lender approval.
List-to-sale ratio
Sale price divided by list price. It helps you see whether homes are closing above, at, or below asking in that micro-market.
Listing agent
The agent marketing the property for the seller. Unless something else is signed, they do not represent your interests.
Loan Estimate
The lender's early official cost estimate. Use it as the baseline for comparing rate, costs, credits, and cash to close.
LTV / CLTV
Loan-to-value and combined loan-to-value. Loan amount compared with property value, often using the lower of price or appraisal.
MLS
Multiple Listing Service. The broker database that feeds many public portals. Public listing copy can lag behind MLS changes.
Net sheet
A seller-side estimate of proceeds after payoff, closing costs, taxes, credits, and fees. FSBO sellers should understand their real net, not just your offer price.
Option fee / due diligence fee
State-specific money paid for a review window. In some places it may be nonrefundable even if you walk away.
PITI
Principal, interest, taxes, and insurance. Add mortgage insurance and HOA dues to get closer to your real monthly carrying cost.
Prepaids and escrows
Upfront taxes, insurance, interest, and reserve deposits. They are not junk fees, but they can move cash to close hard.
Procuring cause
Brokerage language about who caused the sale. If you tour through an agent, understand whether that creates commission drama later.
Proration
A split of taxes, dues, rent, or fees by date. Tiny-looking prorations can move real cash at closing.
Rate buydown
Money paid to lower your mortgage rate. Compare the monthly savings against the cost and how long you expect to keep the loan.
Repair credit
Seller money in place of repairs. Cleaner than seller-managed repairs when allowed, but it must fit lender limits.
Seller concession
Money the seller contributes toward buyer costs. Useful, but lenders cap how much can be credited.
Seller credit vs price cut
A credit helps cash to close. A price cut lowers the loan math. They are not interchangeable if cash is your constraint.
Survey exception
Title language that can leave boundary, encroachment, easement, or improvement-location risk on you if not cleared.
Time is of the essence
A deadline phrase with teeth. If the contract says it, being late can mean losing rights, leverage, or money.
Title commitment
The title company's promise with exceptions. Read the exceptions: easements, restrictions, liens, and survey issues live there.
Title issue
A problem with ownership, liens, boundaries, easements, or claims. Do not treat title language as decoration.
Transaction broker
A limited-role broker model in some states. It is not the same as having a fiduciary buyer advocate.
Walk-away posture
Your willingness to leave if the numbers or risk get bad. It is leverage because it is real, not because you threaten it.

Simple buyer math

The numbers people actually want to know.

You do not need to become a lender. You need fast checks for monthly payment, cash to close, credit value, gap risk, repair leverage, and whether a “deal” actually helps you.

Conventional IPCsCommon Fannie Mae caps: 3%, 6%, or 9% for primary/second homes depending on LTV; 2% for investment property.
FHA concessionsSeller or interested-party contributions are commonly capped at 6% of the lesser of price or value.
VA concessionsSeller concessions above 4% of reasonable value are generally treated as excessive for VA-guaranteed loans.
The buyer trapA credit can be capped by loan rules, actual costs, appraisal value, or whether the item is allowed at all.
What will I really pay each month? real monthly = P&I + taxes + insurance + MI + HOA + maintenance buffer

Principal and interest alone is the pretty number. Carrying cost is the number you live with.

How much cash do I need? cash to close = down payment + closing costs + prepaids - earnest money - credits

This is the number to protect if you are cash constrained. Compare the Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure.

How much of the credit can I actually use? usable credit = min(requested credit, allowed lender cap, actual closing costs/prepaids)

A $15,000 credit is not useful if your loan program or costs only allow part of it.

Is a credit better than a price cut? rough monthly savings ≈ price cut × rate / 12

A $10,000 price cut may only save a small monthly amount. A $10,000 credit may matter more if closing cash is the bottleneck.

What is my appraisal-gap risk? gap exposure = purchase price - appraised value - gap cap

If the number is above zero, the deal needs a price change, more cash, seller movement, or a cancellation path.

Can the loan still qualify? DTI = monthly debt payments / gross monthly income

New debts, HOA dues, insurance jumps, or rate changes can push an approval into trouble.

Is the rate buydown worth it? months to break even = upfront buydown cost / monthly savings

If you might refinance or sell before the break-even point, the lower rate may be less valuable than a credit.

What does the seller actually net? seller net = price - credits - payoff - seller closing costs - taxes/fees

FSBO sellers care about net. A lower price with fewer credits can beat a higher price with messy concessions.

How much should I ask for repairs? repair ask = contractor quote + urgency/risk buffer - items you accept

Anchor repair asks to written quotes. Unsupported round numbers invite weak counters.

How much room is in the list price? list-to-sale % = sale price / list price

If similar homes close at 96% of list, a full-price offer may need a reason beyond vibes.

How much cash is tied up if I walk late? deposit at risk = earnest money + nonrefundable fees + inspection/appraisal spend

This is the pain number. Know it before contingency deadlines expire.

How much loan risk does a low appraisal create? LTV = loan amount / lower of price or appraised value

If appraisal value drops, LTV can rise. That can affect approval, mortgage insurance, or required cash.

Rule-of-thumb examples are not underwriting approval. Conventional IPC caps can vary by occupancy and LTV, FHA and VA have their own concession rules, and your lender/title company controls final treatment.
